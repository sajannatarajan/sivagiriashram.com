The Revolutionary Consecrations

Guru consecrated temples open to all — a radical act that transformed Kerala society

In 19th-century Kerala, lower-caste Hindus were barred from entering temples. Sree Narayana Guru broke this oppression by consecrating temples himself — without Brahmin priests — and declaring them open to all people, regardless of caste.

He consecrated over 60 temples across Kerala, installing deities of different traditions — Shiva, Vishnu, Devi, Saraswati — but always with the same message: this temple belongs to all humanity. God does not discriminate.

Sivagiri Mutt & Sarada Mutt

ശിവഗിരി മഠം
📍 Varkala, Thiruvananthapuram District, Kerala
Mahasamadhi Shrine Pilgrimage Site Founded 1904 Deity: Sarada Devi

Sivagiri Mutt in Varkala is the most sacred site associated with Sree Narayana Guru — the place of his final abode and Mahasamadhi (conscious departure from the body, 1928). It is the spiritual headquarters of the Sree Narayana movement worldwide.

The Mutt houses the Sarada Mutt, dedicated to Sarada Devi (the goddess of knowledge). It also contains Guru's samadhi shrine, where devotees come to pay their respects and receive blessings.

The annual Sivagiri Pilgrimage (Sivagiri Teertha Yatra), inaugurated by Guru himself in 1904, draws hundreds of thousands of pilgrims every December 30–January 1.

Pilgrimage Information:
  • Annual pilgrimage: December 30 – January 1
  • Nearest railway station: Varkala Sivagiri (SNCF)
  • Temple open to all faiths
  • Prasadam distributed daily

Aruvippuram Shiva Temple

അരുവിപ്പുറം ക്ഷേത്രം
📍 Aruvippuram, Neyyattinkara, Thiruvananthapuram
First Consecration — 1888 Historic Site Deity: Shiva

Aruvippuram holds the most historic significance of all sites associated with Sree Narayana Guru. It was here, on the banks of the Neyyar river, that Guru performed his first temple consecration in 1888 — the consecration that changed Kerala's history.

Guru consecrated the Shiva idol by himself, without Brahmin priests, and declared: "This is not the Brahmin's Shiva — this is the Shiva of all people." It was the first time in Kerala's history that a non-Brahmin had consecrated a Shiva temple.

The consecration sparked what became known as the Aruvippuram Declaration — a proclamation that lower-caste Hindus had the same right to worship as any other Hindu. This event galvanised Kerala's social reform movement.

Shivratri Festival: The annual Shivratri festival at Aruvippuram is celebrated with special poojas, recitations, and a re-enactment of the historic consecration ceremony.

Kalavankode Sree Narayana Mutt

കളവൻകോഡ് മഠം
📍 Kalavankode, Thiruvananthapuram
Mutt Educational Institution Welfare Centre

Kalavankode Mutt was one of the first institutions established under Guru's direct guidance. It served as a centre for spiritual practice, education, and social welfare — embodying Guru's vision of the integration of spiritual and social development.

Kalavilakom Sree Narayana Guru Smarakam

ഗുരു സ്മാരകം
📍 Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala
Memorial Museum Research Centre

This memorial and research centre is dedicated to preserving and propagating the life, teachings, and works of Sree Narayana Guru. It houses a museum with artefacts, photographs, and manuscripts from Guru's life.

The Mirror Temple — Murukkumpuzha

ദർപ്പണ ക്ഷേത്രം
📍 Murukkumpuzha, Thiruvananthapuram
Unique — Mirror Idol Philosophical Teaching

In one of his most profound and enigmatic acts, Guru consecrated a temple where instead of a conventional idol, he installed a large mirror. The inscription read: "ഇവിടെ ദൈവദർശനം" — "Here, behold the vision of God."

When worshippers looked into the mirror, they saw themselves. The teaching was immediate and inescapable: the divine you seek is your own true nature. This was Advaita philosophy made directly experiential — the most radical form of worship possible.

Temples Across South India

Maruthvamala Temple

📍 Maruthvamala Hills, Thiruvananthapuram

One of Guru's most important tapas (ascetic practice) sites. He spent years here in deep meditation before returning to public life as a reformer. A temple now marks the site of his prolonged solitary meditation.

Thrissur Advaita Ashram

📍 Thrissur, Kerala

An ashram founded under Guru's influence, dedicated to Advaita Vedanta practice and social service. Guru visited here and gave discourses that shaped the local reform movement.

Kanyakumari Mutt

📍 Kanyakumari, Tamil Nadu

At the southernmost tip of India, where three seas meet, Guru's influence extended into Tamil Nadu. The Kanyakumari Mutt carries his legacy of open worship and social equality.

Mangalore Sree Narayana Temple

📍 Mangalore, Karnataka

Guru's influence reached Karnataka, and several temples in the Tulu Nadu region carry his vision of open, caste-free worship open to all.

Vadakara Sree Narayana Dharma Paripalana Temple

📍 Vadakara, Kozhikode

One of the major SNDP-affiliated temples in northern Kerala, continuing Guru's mission of universal worship and social equality in the Malabar region.

Alathur Sree Narayana Mission Temple

📍 Alathur, Palakkad

Central Kerala's Narayana Guru temple, serving as a centre for education, social welfare, and continued propagation of Guru's message of human equality.

Institutions Founded

Temples, Mutts, Schools, Hospitals — Guru's vision made concrete

Sree Narayana Dharma Paripalana Yogam (SNDP)

Founded in 1903 with Guru's blessings, the SNDP Yogam was the organisational vehicle for Guru's social mission. It grew to become one of the largest and most powerful social organisations in Kerala, fighting against caste discrimination and promoting education, welfare, and equality.

Today the SNDP oversees hundreds of temples, schools, colleges, hospitals, and welfare organisations across Kerala and the Kerala diaspora worldwide.

Educational Institutions

Guru established or inspired the founding of dozens of schools and educational centres for communities that had been denied education under the caste system. His schools were among the first in Kerala to admit students of all castes and social backgrounds.

The educational movement he started contributed directly to Kerala achieving near-universal literacy — one of the highest literacy rates in the developing world.
